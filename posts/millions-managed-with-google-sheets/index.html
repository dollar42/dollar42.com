I manage a large, long-term investment portfolio of index funds with a Google Sheet.

I was embarrassed about it. Now I embrace it. I think it is pretty good.

If The Spreadsheet and its impact on my financial life interests you, please subscribe for future posts via email or RSS.

The Spreadsheet helps answer over time:

  1. What do I own in all of my accounts across all types (taxable, tax-free, tax-deferred; employer, IRA, etc.)?
  2. What is my target asset allocation across my entire portfolio?
  3. How closely does reality match that target across my entire portfolio?

And, of course: What do I do if reality does not match?

The Spreadsheet does not need much information. Currently there are input sheets for:

  • Account names and types.
  • Fund symbols and the asset class(es) they represent.
  • Holdings/transactions: a fund and the number of shares I own per account (e.g., FUND1, 50.12 shares in my Roth IRA).

With the power of the GOOGLEFINANCE function, The Spreadsheet automatically produces an Account Holdings report showing accounts, holdings and balances:

I use this report to crosscheck the balances and holdings against my online statements. If something is too different, I probably missed entering a transaction.

I also print this report to include with my estate plan. I hope it will aid my spouse or executor when they need to inventory accounts after I die.

The Spreadsheet now shifts to asset allocation1. Asset allocation is probably the most important decision an investor makes.

I codify my asset allocation with variables and formulas that show the thinking behind the ultimate percentages:

In this example portfolio, Alice and Bob create an asset allocation that:

  • Reserves $100,000 as a cash buffer off the top.
  • Invests the rest 65% in stocks (and by subtraction 35% in bonds).
  • Invests 60% of the stock allocation in US stocks (and by subtraction 40% in non-US stocks).
  • Invests 30% of the bond allocation in TIPS2 (and by subtraction 70% in nominal/nom. bonds).

In cell C9, $100,000 is automatically calculated as 4.57% of their roughly $2.2 million portfolio. In the other cells in column C, the allocations to all other asset classes are also automatically adjusted downward to compensate for the cash buffer.

The variables on the right are entirely editable. In other experiments with The Spreadsheet, I have changed them to be completely different.

With the asset allocation in place, The Spreadsheet automatically produces its second report, the Rebalance Matrix:

This report places accounts on the left and asset classes across the top.

Each cell on an account row is a fund in that account in the correct asset class column (e.g., VTI in cell B12 is in the US Stocks column whereas VXUS in cell C14 is in the Int’l Stocks column).

Different funds in the same asset class are “stacked” vertically automatically (e.g., cells B20-B23 where 65% of VT and 100% of VTI are allocated to US Stocks within the Brokerage (Joint) account). 35% of VT, its non-US allocation, is shown in cell C20.

Back toward the top in rows 5-10, Target ($ and %) and Actual show target and actual allocations for each asset class summed by column, representing the actual allocation across all accounts.

If target and actual deviate too much, the Difference cells will become more yellow and eventually red, indicating that money should ideally move from the overperforming asset class to the underperforming one (“sell high, buy low”).

Alice and Bob probably do not need to rebalance! They are within 1 percentage point of their desired asset allocation in every asset class. The largest deviation is shown in cell D7/D10.

But what if I tweak a variable in the Asset Allocation sheet? Let’s consider if Alice and Bob decide to increase their stock allocation to 75% (from 65%):

Alice and Bob may want to start moving money out of bonds and into stocks in this scenario.

For example, US stocks are 5.24 percentage points (cell B7) from target. $114,579 (cell B10) needs to flow from other asset classes into US Stocks to rebalance exactly.

Rebalancing can be a complicated exercise that involves information The Spreadsheet does not have, such as future financial goals, unrealized gain amounts in taxable accounts, fund availability in retirement plans, desire to keep the total number of funds small, etc.

However, The Spreadsheet makes it easier than other tools I have tried.

Here, I go to the Transactions tab and make two new entries:

These rows represent an exchange Alice could consider:

  1. A sale of $115,000 (negative shares) of FXNAX, a bond fund. The portfolio is currently overweight Nom. Bonds.
  2. With the proceeds, a purchase of $115,000 (positive shares) of FSKAX, a stock fund. The portfolio is currently underweight US Stocks.

I did not calculate these share counts by hand. Instead I used =115000 / GOOGLEFINANCE("SYMBOL") to get the rough number of shares for $115,000 at today’s prices. For the sale, I used -115000, a negative number.

Because I am experimenting, ballpark figures are OK. If Alice executed this trade, she would change the values to the exact share counts after the trade settled.

Adding these two transactions causes the Rebalance Matrix to automatically regenerate3:

In cells B16-D17, the exchange is shown as $115,000 of newly purchased FSKAX (the stock fund) while FXNAX (the bond fund) shows a balance reduced by $115,000 compared to the prior screenshot.

The Difference columns for US Stocks and Nom. Bonds are noticably more green. This exchange would help get the portfolio closer to alignment with its new target.

There could be more to do with this example portfolio, but that should give you an idea of how I use The Spreadsheet.

Summary

I think long-term investing requires:

  1. Creating a plan.
  2. Sticking to the plan.
  3. Ignoring noise from investors playing a different (short-term) game along the way.

The Spreadsheet is an attempt to give useful information to long-term investors, without the noise that most tools spam along the way.

There is intentionally no information about performance or the ability to manage dozens of securities and frequent trading. The Spreadsheet is not the tool for those portfolios because those portfolios are unlikely to be good for long-term investors.

A modern portfolio sized well into the millions of dollars may consist only of a few funds and a few accounts. Simplicity is desirable. Complexity can be a way to hide nefarious behavior and high fees.
